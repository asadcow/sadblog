Looking Back: WEB Orientation

AUG 8 2024

I still can't believe I'm starting high school in a week. Over the summer, I played golf with people who were 1 or 2 years older than me, and some of them have asked me if I'm excited for high school to start.

I suppose it would be nice to have some structure in my life again. To compensate for waking up at 6 AM for golf tournaments, I've been sleeping until 9 on other days. But I'm also worried about the workload. I didn't have many classes this summer, and I've spent most of my time immersing myself in Olympic gymnastics and Minecraft.

My brother is starting middle school, and he has orientation tomorrow. I don't remember much from the start of 6th grade. However, I found this piece that I wrote two hours after my "second" middle school orientation happened. What does that mean? Well, I was a part of WEB—Where Everybody Belongs. It's a middle school transition program where the trained WEB leaders, who are 8th graders, facilitate activities during orientation and other events.

------------------------------

WEB: Where Everybody Belongs, Faces their Fears, and Avoids the G-Word

[This is not the original writing. Some sections have been revised as needed.]

On the Friday before school started, over a hundred nervous or maybe excited 6th graders walked into the school gym for their first taste of middle school: orientation. Obviously, I went through 6th grade orientation too. Maybe they didn't know, but I felt the same emotions because we were leading an event I had never experienced myself…

During the pandemic when we couldn't have 200 people in one room, we didn't get to walk under a cheering human tunnel while entering the gym. And I've never had the opportunity to be part of organizing a major welcoming event. But when our school announced the WEB application in the spring of 7th grade, I felt the need to join so I could finally do something to benefit our school's community while developing leadership skills. Being a WEB leader felt like the perfect way to practice. I remembered how lost I felt on the first day of school without my elementary school friends and wanted to make sure the 6th graders didn't feel too confused.

The first thirty minutes of orientation were held in the gym, which was covered in resurrected welcome signs. We, the WEB leaders and 6th graders, met each other in fun ways (“say everything you did in the last 24 hours, but in reverse order”) and watched ten brave volunteers participate in a relay race of popping balloons.

After that segment, we broke off into our groups and led the students to our assigned classrooms. We took turns leading different activities, and each exercise had an intended purpose, including handling teamwork challenges, learning about classmates, and discussing strategies for school success. Training for this took the longest. To see what the games looked like, we, the WEB leaders, took the position of the students, and the WEB advisors read the instructions to us. After these initial introductions came practice—lots of it. We knew the activities so well that we kept doing the next step before the instructions were read. However, the 6th graders wouldn't know the activities, and we had to be mindful of that while practicing.

During training, we were reminded not to use the word “game” when describing these activities, since “game” implies a winner and a loser. However, this was difficult for some of the activities, including One Left, which was structured just like a game. This activity used 15 straws, and the objective was to take turns picking up either 1, 2, or 3 straws so that you don't pick up the last straw. The leader was supposed to play “against” the 6th graders and win each time because we knew the strategy. Afterward, we would reveal the specific strategy and then play a few more rounds, showing how strategies could be helpful. However, [partner 1] kept calling the activity a game. We even got the 6th graders to laugh and remind him to say “activity” instead.

All of the activities were designed to lead into the next one. So as a joke, I announced, “Ok, so now we are going to move onto the next lecture—oh no, I meant activity—where we will be discussing success strategies for middle school!” The students received their schedules and planners during this activity. During training, we looked through their schedules so we could show the 6th graders their classroom locations. We were reminded not to call any teacher “good” or “bad,” as “none of the teachers are bad. They just teach in different ways” -WEB advisors.

The next part of orientation was the campus tour led by WEB leaders. The leaders of each group planned routes that showed the location of every classroom and other key places like the lunch lines, library, and the blacktop. When we encountered other groups, we yelled our unique chant, created by [partner 1]: “Ooga booga, ooga booga, ooga booga, EDINBURGH!” After a few of these exchanges, part of me wished that we had just created a normal chant like everyone else, e.g., “ALCAZAR! ALCAZAR! ALCAZAR!” I'm pretty sure [partner 2] felt the same.

Besides all the practicing of our small group roles and hosting the actual event, I finally experienced the thoughtful preparation went in behind the scenes. For example, we helped to hang up welcoming posters and set up check-in tables in the gym. We also met other WEB leaders and built a strong team by playing games—no wait, they're activities. In fact, most of the first training day revolved around team building.

Another task we completed was to (phone) call each sixth grader in our groups to remind them about orientation. I'm always overly worried about calling the wrong number, and it did not help that we were supposed to talk directly with kids not much younger than us in an adult's tone and without scaring them. Nevertheless, I did eventually face my fears.

Overall, being part of orientation was a memorable and enjoyable experience. I learned the tremendous efforts others invest to prepare for successful school events, and I got to spend time around other people with leadership qualities. I hope that by being a part of WEB, I can learn how to talk with others.
